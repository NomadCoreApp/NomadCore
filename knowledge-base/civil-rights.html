### Procedure: Describe the Admissibility of Evidence from Illegal Search or Inspection

Overview: This procedure outlines how to describe the admissibility of evidence obtained through an illegal search or inspection in a trial.

#### Key Points:
• Illegal searches and inspections do not automatically make evidence inadmissible, but they may be excluded under certain legal standards.
• Evidence obtained from an illegal search may be deemed inadmissible if it violates the 4th Amendment protections against unreasonable searches and seizures.
• Courts use a balancing test to determine whether the evidence should be excluded, considering factors such as the severity of the violation and the importance of the evidence to the case.

#### Legal Standards:
• The exclusionary rule generally prohibits the use of evidence obtained in violation of the 4th Amendment.
• However, there are exceptions, such as when the evidence is independent of the illegal search or if it was obtained through a good faith exception (e.g., reliance on a warrant later found to be invalid).

#### Application:
• This procedure applies in military justice contexts and requires understanding how the U.S. Constitution’s 4th Amendment protections are applied within the Army.
• The admissibility of evidence is evaluated based on whether it was obtained through unreasonable or illegal means, regardless of its relevance to the case.

#### Notes:
• This procedure is part of a broader evaluation on understanding and applying constitutional rights in military justice.
• It is critical for Soldiers to understand how evidence is treated in legal proceedings, especially when dealing with searches and inspections conducted without proper authorization.

### Procedure: Describe the Authority for Search and Seizure under the 4th Amendment

Overview: This procedure outlines how to describe the authority for search and seizure, including the protections of the U.S. Constitution’s 4th Amendment, its application in the U.S. Army, and the necessity for it to apply differently to Soldiers and civilians.

#### Key Points:
• The 4th Amendment protects individuals from unreasonable searches and seizures by government officials.
• It requires that any search or seizure be based on probable cause and supported by a warrant or authorization in the Armed Forces.
• This protection is essential to ensure privacy rights are upheld, but it may apply differently to Soldiers compared to civilians due to the unique nature of military service.

#### Application in the U.S. Army:
• The 4th Amendment applies to U.S. Army Soldiers, but its application may be modified by military regulations and commander authority.
• In some cases, such as during disciplinary actions or administrative inspections, the protections of the 4th Amendment may be less strict compared to civilian contexts.

#### Necessity for Different Application:
• The military environment often requires a balance between individual rights and military discipline and security needs.
• This can lead to exceptions, such as when a commander has authority over the place being searched, or during exigent circumstances that require immediate action.

#### Notes:

• Soldiers must be aware of how these rights apply to them and when they may be subject to searches or seizures under different legal standards.

Understanding Government Roles in Property Protection

The government plays a crucial role in protecting both individuals and their property. This protection is achieved through three main branches:

1. Legislative Branch: Composed of elected officials who create laws to govern society.
2. Executive Branch: Includes leaders such as the President, governors, and mayors who are responsible for enforcing these laws.
3. Judicial Branch: Consists of judges and court officers who interpret and apply the laws in a fair manner.

It is a fundamental principle that no single individual or group should hold authority over all three branches of government to ensure checks and balances.

The Constitution of the United States, as well as state constitutions and city charters, reflects this division into legislative, executive, and judicial departments.
