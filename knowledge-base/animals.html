Procedure for Optimal Hive Orientation and Placement During Winter:

1. Hive orientation when left outdoors:
- Position hives to face north so that the sun does not shine directly on the entrance.
- This prevents bees from being tempted out during cold weather, which could lead to loss of individuals due to inability to fly safely.

2. Secure hive entrances:
- Ensure all entrances are closed securely against mice and other pests.

3. Hive type selection for outdoor wintering:
- Use upright hive forms, such as the American style, when storing bees outdoors during winter.
- This allows better clustering of bees and more efficient heat retention.

4. Ensure sufficient honey stores:
- Provide at least 30 lbs. of honey per hive to ensure adequate food supply for the winter months.

5. Avoid overfeeding or underfeeding:
- Too much honey may lead to unnecessary consumption, while too little can result in starvation.
- The goal is to provide enough honey to last until swarming time in spring.

Procedure for Wintering Bees Successfully:

1. Understand bee behavior in cold weather:
- Bees cluster together to maintain body heat.
- In the coldest days, their cluster can reach summer temperatures internally.
- They remain in a semi-torpid state during winter and consume very little food.

2. Ensure proper ventilation:
- Provide upward ventilation in hives to allow warm air to rise from the bee cluster.
- In shallow hives, this is critical for keeping stores above the cluster.

3. Provide sufficient food:
- Ensure bees have access to plenty of honey (at least 30 lbs. per hive).
- Remove honey boxes under the cap to allow upward ventilation.

4. Maintain adequate numbers of bees:
- A large enough population is necessary to maintain body heat in cold weather.

5. Choose appropriate storage location:
- If possible, store hives in a cellar during winter for better temperature control and reduced honey consumption.
- In the North, this should be done from about the last of November until spring.
- In the Middle and Southern States, bees should be housed from when bloom ceases until it returns.

6. Position hives correctly:
- If left outside during winter, position them to face north so that sunlight does not tempt bees out on cold days.
- Secure hive entrances against mice.

7. Timing for moving bees back outdoors:
- Choose a warm, pleasant day in spring to move bees back outdoors.
- Bees will be attracted by light and may fly out if the weather is still chilly, risking loss of some individuals.

8. Ensure hives are heavy when set out in spring:
- Hives should have enough honey stored to last until swarming time.
- This ensures bees can rear young rapidly during the spring months and be ready for the harvest season.

Procedure for Wintering Bees in Cellars:

1. Timing of cellar placement:
- In the Northern States, place bees in cellars around the last of November when cold weather begins.
- In the Middle and Southern States, house bees from when bloom ceases until it returns.

2. Cellar storage benefits:
- Bees consume significantly less honey when stored in a cellar compared to being outdoors.
- Example: A hive weighing 60 lbs. in fall lost only 5 lbs. over three months in a cellar, while one left outdoors lost 29 1/2 lbs. over the same period.

3. Cellar storage conditions:
- Ensure proper ventilation within the cellar for the hives.
- Avoid placing bees in cellars too early before cold weather is established.

4. Hive preparation for cellar storage:
- Use upright hive forms, such as the American style, if not storing in a cellar.
- Ensure honey boxes under the cap are removed to allow upward ventilation.

5. Cellar storage duration:
- In the North, bees may be stored in cellars for three to five months.

6. Reintroducing bees outdoors in spring:
- Choose a warm day when reintroducing bees to outdoor stands.
- Bees will be attracted by light and may fly out if the weather is still chilly, risking loss of some individuals.

- Repeat oil and clysters if constipation persists.

Procedure:

1. Fast the horse: Withhold all food for one day.
2. Prepare a warm bran mash and give it to the horse at night, followed by a medicinal ball made of:
- 1 scruple of calomel
- 1 scruple of turpeth mineral
- Crumb of bread and honey (enough to form a mass)
3. Next evening, administer:
- 1 pint of castor oil
- ½ pint of linseed oil
4. Resume normal feeding for two or three days after the treatment.
5. Repeat this process as needed.

Alternative remedies (fall season):
• Give a drench of sweetened milk followed by brine (4–5 ounces salt to 1 quart water).
• Administer oil of turpentine (4 oz) in a pint of gruel, after fasting the horse.
• Use nux vomica (dog-buttons):
- Rasp one nut and pour a pint of boiling water over it.
- Cool to blood heat and drench the horse with it.
- Bleed the horse in the mouth about 30 minutes before administration to encourage worm expulsion.

Elk or Wapiti (Cervus canadensis)

• Smaller than the moose.
• Height at the shoulder: 4 to 5 feet.
• Weight: 400 to 800 pounds.

Procedure:

1. Examine physical features:
- Look for a small body size with short fur.
- Note the coloration: generally light brown above, with a cream-colored throat and belly, and a black-tipped tail.
- In northern areas, observe if they have a white winter coat, which identifies them as ermine (a subspecies of weasels).

2. Measure size:
- Long-tailed weasels (Mustela frenata) measure 13 to 17 inches in length, including a 4.5 to 6.5 inch tail.
- Short-tailed weasels (Mustela erminea) have tails of 4 inches or less.

3. Compare sex differences:
- Males are generally larger than females.

4. Determine geographic distribution:
- Long-tailed weasels are found in most U.S. states except the southwest.
- Short-tailed weasels are primarily located in northern states.

5. Assess habitat preferences:
- Weasels live in a variety of environments including mountains, farmland, forests, and prairies near water.

6. Note behavior:
- They are primarily nocturnal and stay within a half-mile radius of their den.

7. Identify prey and predators:
- Prey includes mice, voles, chipmunks, rabbits, birds, eggs, and poultry.
- Predators include foxes, mink, coyotes, bobcats, hawks, and owls.

8. Observe den characteristics:
- Dens are located in hollow stumps, tree roots, rock piles, or under old buildings.
- They are lined with grasses and fur from prey animals.

9. Understand reproductive behavior:
- Breeding occurs in July, with delayed implantation.
- One litter of an average of six young is produced.
- Females mate at 3 to 4 months old, while males typically mate during their second year of life.

How to change Colonies of Black Bees to Italians:

Since the queen is the mother of all the bees in the hive, and deposits all the eggs, it follows that they will all be like her. If then the queen be taken from a colony of common bees, and an Italian queen be put there in her stead, all the eggs thenceforth laid will produce Italian bees, and as the life of the worker bee is short, in from two to three months the old bees will all have died out and be replaced in greater numbers by the beautiful Italians.

These Italian queens are now reared for sale by scientific apiarians, and sent to any part of the world with perfect safety. If a pure queen purely impregnated is purchased and introduced to any colony of black bees, an Italian stock is secured in the best and least expensive way. Italian queens impregnated by common drones will produce what is called "hybrid" stock, and though this is a great improvement on the black bees, it is very desirable to secure a queen to commence with that is warranted pure by some reliable person.

Many bee-keepers have, by the purchase of one queen, succeeded in Italianizing all their stocks to their great profit and pleasure.

How to introduce an Italian Queen to a Native Colony in the Safest Way:

A colony have a great attachment for their queen being, it would seem, conscious that on her presence their whole existence depends. When she is taken from them, some precautions are necessary, lest they destroy a strange queen presented to them. There are various ways of doing this, but the following will be found safe and easy.

1. Remove the existing queen:
- Take away the queen of the colony to which the Italian is to be given.
- To find her most easily, open the stand in the middle of a fine day, when many bees are absent from the hive.
- Disturb the bees as little as possible, and have an assistant to look on one side of the frame, while you examine the other.
- Look first on the combs near where the bees cluster, as the queen is apt to be there.

2. Prepare the Italian queen for introduction:
- As soon as you have found and killed her, put the Italian queen, with three or more of the bees that come with her, into a wire cage which always accompanies her when sent, and lay this over the frames near the cluster, or, if the weather be cool, the cage may be laid between two frames.
- Leave her there for 48 hours, and then, without disturbing the bees, withdraw the stopper and allow the queen to go into the hive at her pleasure.

3. Handle the queen carefully:
- Open the box in which the queen is sent in a light room, that, if the queen fly, she may be caught on the window.
- Never handle a queen by the abdomen, as a pressure there may be fatal; take her by the upper part of the body or wings.

4. Move and prepare the hive:
- Move the hive to which you wish to introduce the queen to a new position some distance away.
- Take from it two or three frames of comb containing brood and honey, shake all the bees from them, put them in an empty hive, and place this hive where the other stood;
- Close all the entrances except one hole in the top, and through this gently put the new queen on to the frames and shut her in.

5. Allow bees to return safely:
- Then open entrance for one bee at a time in front, and allow the bees that are out in the fields to come in. Being full of honey, and disturbed also at the new appearance of their home, they will not harm the stranger queen.

6. Optional: Create new colonies:
- If it is the right season of the year for making new colonies, you may, towards night, add a frame or two more of brood from some other colony.
- As a majority of the worker trees from the removed colony will return to their new location, you will have a good, new, prosperous colony.

7. Alternative method:
- If you do not care thus to divide, look over the removed hive at once and kill its queen.
- Leave it an hour or two, and then take all the remaining frames, shake the bees from them and place them in the hive where your Italian queen is,
- Just at dusk put all the remaining bees in the old hive, in front of the new, which they will gladly enter. In this way you remove by degrees all parts of a colony to a new hive, except its queen, which you replace by the Italian.

This is my favorite way of exchanging queens.

To determine the age of a dog until it is six years old, examine its teeth:

1. Teeth Inspection: Look at the four front teeth in both the upper and lower jaws, with particular attention to those in the upper jaw.
2. Age Indicators:
- At 1 year old: The four front teeth are jagged and uneven, resembling a fleur de lis but not as pointed.
- Between 3-4 years old: These marks will be about half worn down.
- Near or at 6 years old: All the four front teeth in both jaws are smooth and even with no jagged edges.
3. Limitations: Once these marks are completely worn flat, it is no longer possible to estimate a dog's age based on its teeth.
4. Common Mistakes: Many hunters incorrectly assess a dog’s age by looking at the side and eye-teeth, which can be misleading due to conditions like canker in the mouth.
