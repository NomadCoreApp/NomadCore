Loss of Adaptability from Stress:

• Panic and confusion may occur when a service member is overwhelmed by danger.
- This can impair mental ability, making it difficult to think clearly or follow simple commands.
- Judgment may be faulty, leading to actions such as standing up in a fighting position during an attack.

• Symptoms resembling head injuries:
- Dazed appearance or aimless wandering.
- Confusion and disorientation.
- Partial or complete loss of memory.

What to Do:
1. If symptoms resemble those of a head injury, rapidly medically evacuate the service member for evaluation.
2. Do not allow the service member to expose themselves to further personal danger until the cause of the problem is determined.
3. Provide immediate support and ensure they are removed from stressful environments if necessary.

Sleep Disturbance and Repetition of Dreams:

• Common effects of stress on sleep:
- Difficulty sleeping.
- Nightmares related to the stressful event.
- These are considered normal in the short term after intense stress.

• Repetitive reexperiencing of events:
- Some service members may repeatedly think about or act out parts of the stressful experience while awake.
- This is a natural part of recovery and should not be discouraged.

What to Do:
1. Encourage the service member to ventilate by talking extensively, even repetitively, about their experience.
2. Provide a safe environment for them to express emotions without judgment.
3. Monitor for signs of disruption in daily life due to these reactions and offer additional support if needed.

Emotional Reactions to Stress:

• Inefficient performance may include:
- Slow thinking or reaction time.
- Difficulty recognizing priorities or seeing what needs to be done.
- Indecisiveness and trouble focusing attention.
- Tendency to perform only familiar tasks and become preoccupied with details.
- Passive behavior, such as sitting or wandering aimlessly without purpose.

• Uncontrolled emotional outbursts may include:
- Crying, screaming, or laughing uncontrollably.
- Withdrawal and isolation from others.

What to Do:
1. Encourage service members who are withdrawn to remain with their unit for support.
2. If a person is experiencing uncontrolled emotional outbursts (e.g., crying one moment and laughing the next), provide reassurance and avoid confrontation.
3. Monitor for signs of restlessness or inappropriate aggression, such as indiscriminately striking others.
4. Ensure the individual is not exposed to further danger until their condition is assessed.

Leaders must be able to recognize the psychological symptoms of combat stress, which can overlap with physical and environmental factors. The following are common signs:

### Mood Changes
• Unusual impatience, frustration, or irritability.
• Unusual fatigue or sleepiness.
• Feelings of loneliness, isolation, or alienation.
• Feelings of helplessness.
• Claustrophobia.

### Thinking and Cognitive Impairment
• Forgetfulness or absentmindedness (especially errors in map plotting and message coding).
• Impaired decision making, reasoning, or judgment.
• Disorientation, confusion, or panic.
• Hallucinations or paranoia.

### Physical Signs
• Rapid breathing (hyperventilation) or rapid heart rate (tachycardia).
• "Cotton mouth" or nausea.
• Cramps or muscle tension.

After ensuring their physical safety, focus on your children’s emotional needs by encouraging them to express their feelings. Drawing pictures and telling stories is a good way to do this. Listen carefully as they share and explain what really happened. Be honest and tell them of any further dangers that may still exist and that you will work it out together.

• As with adults, hugs are reassuring; give them liberally! Tell them things will be better again but don’t lie.

• Include children in any necessary cleanup activities. Having a job to do refocuses their attention on something more positive and creates a more comforting, normal environment.

• It is common for children to revert to immature behaviour for a few days. Some normal reactions are irritability, anger, sadness, diarrhea, headache, nightmares, and loss of appetite. Praise them for sharing their feelings and for helping around the house rather than focusing on this behaviour. However, if it persists for more than two weeks, seek professional advice.

• Be aware that for a time aspects of the disaster can trigger their fears (such as seeing flames or smelling smoke if there was a fire). Talk through their fear and reassure them they are safe.

• Return to a normal schedule as soon as possible but relax the rules for a while.

• Try to spend a little extra time with them, especially at bedtime.

• Work with their teachers, childcare givers, babysitters and others who need to understand how the disaster has affected your children.

Normal stress reactions are common after traumatic events and include:

• Feeling shaky, sweaty, nauseated, or confused.
• These reactions are normal and not a cause for concern.

However, some short- or long-term reactions may require attention if left unchecked. See paragraph 8-13 for more information.

Emotional Reactions:

The most obvious combat stress reaction (CSR) is inefficient performance, demonstrated by:

1. Slow thinking or reaction time.
2. Difficulty recognizing priorities and seeing what needs to be done.
3. Difficulty getting started.
4. Indecisiveness and trouble focusing attention.
5. Tendency to do familiar tasks and be preoccupied with familiar details (may become very passive, such as sitting or wandering about not knowing what to do).

A less common reaction may be uncontrolled emotional outbursts, demonstrated by:

• Crying, screaming, or laughing.
• Some service members may become withdrawn and silent, trying to isolate themselves from others. These individuals should be encouraged to remain with their assigned unit.

Uncontrolled reactions may appear alone or in any combination (e.g., crying uncontrollably one minute and then laughing the next). In this state, the person is restless and cannot keep still. They may run about without purpose. Inside, they feel a great rage or fear, and their physical acts may show this. In anger, they may indiscriminately strike out at others.

Panic and Confusion:

In a desperate attempt to get away from danger, a service member may panic and become confused. Their mental ability may be so impaired that they cannot think clearly or follow simple commands. Judgment may be faulty, and they may not be aware of their actions (e.g., standing up in their fighting position during an attack).

Symptoms Similar to Head Injury:

Overwhelming stress may produce symptoms associated with head injuries, such as:

• Appearing dazed or wandering aimlessly.
• Confusion and disorientation.
• Complete or partial loss of memory.

In these cases, especially when no eyewitnesses can confirm the service member has not suffered a head injury, it is necessary for them to be rapidly medically evacuated. DO NOT allow the service member to expose themselves to further personal danger until the cause of the problem has been determined.

Objective: Recognize and manage psychological reactions that may arise during survival situations.

Steps:
1. Understand common internal reactions such as fear, anxiety, anger, frustration, depression, loneliness, boredom, and guilt.
2. Manage fear by being cautious in dangerous situations to avoid injury.
3. Reduce anxiety by performing tasks that ensure survival (e.g., securing shelter, finding food and water).
4. Address anger and frustration by focusing on problem-solving rather than irrational behavior.
5. Combat depression by maintaining a sense of purpose and seeking ways to stay mentally engaged.
6. Alleviate loneliness and boredom by keeping the mind occupied with productive activities.
7. Deal with guilt by acknowledging it but not allowing it to prevent living or taking action.
