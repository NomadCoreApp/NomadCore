Materials Needed for Construction:

• Two large, shallow corrugated cardboard (carton board) boxes that are nestable:
- An INNER BOX at least 45x55 centimeters (18x22 inches), preferably just a little bit taller than your pots.
- An OUTER BOX a little larger in all dimensions so there is at least 3-5 centimeters (1-2 inches) space between the two boxes on all sides when nested. Ideal proportions: one unit tall by two units long (front to back) by three units wide (side to side).

• Corrugated cardboard (carton board) at least 15 centimeters (6 inches) longer and wider than the outer box to make the lid.
• Window glass at least 50x60 centimeters (20x24 inches) and longer and wider than the inner box.
• Thin, black metal sheet, sized equal to or slightly smaller than the inner box.
• Aluminum foil — 0.3x20 meters (1x60 feet), cut into strips as needed.
• Dry plant fibers or about 50 sheets of newspaper, quartered and loosely crumpled.
• Glue (nontoxic, water-based).
• Silicone caulk.
• Rigid wire (hanger wire, etc.), or a stick and string — 0.7 meters (2 feet) each.
• Paintbrush (the foam type work well).
• Utility knife or similar cutting device.
• Pencil, pen, or other marking device.
• Large ruler or other straight edge.

Notes:
• Cookers that are too tall create shadows across the pot and increase heat loss through walls.
• See page 26 for substitute materials.

For a noontime meal:
1. Face east: Orient the front of the cooker (opposite the reflector) toward the east, or where the sun will be mid-morning.
2. Start cooking early: Begin cooking by 9:00 or 10:00 a.m. for best results.

For an evening meal:
1. Face west: Orient the front of the cooker toward the west, where the sun will be mid-afternoon.
2. Start cooking in the afternoon: Begin cooking by 1:00 or 2:00 p.m. for best results.

For all-day cooking:
1. Face noon position: Orient the cooker so that it faces where the sun will be at noon or early afternoon.
2. Food ready in the evening: This setup ensures food is ready by evening mealtime.

General tips:
• Food cooks fastest when the shadow created by the cooker is directly behind it.
• For longer cooking times or large quantities, reorient the cooker every couple of hours to speed up the process slightly.

To prepare dark cooking pots for solar cooking:

1. Select Pot: Use lidded, dark-colored pots or similar vessels. Thin metal pots work best.
2. Alternative Vessels: Tinted glass baking dishes, heavy metal, ceramic, or clay pots can also be used, but they absorb heat more slowly.
3. Painting Pots:
- If you don’t have dark-colored pots and lids, paint them black (outer surfaces only).
- Use dull, nontoxic latex or blackboard paints for best results.
- If using oil-based paint, bake the pot in the sun for several days to eliminate odor.
4. Alternative Paint Options:
- Water-based glue mixed with soot or poster paint can be used but is less durable.
5. Glass Jars: Use glass jars with lids if painted black. Place a vertical strip of tape on the jar before painting and remove it to leave a space for viewing food during cooking.
6. Recommended Lids: Canning jars and lids are recommended because they release excessive steam pressure when needed.

General Tips:

• Solar cooking is not an exact science — many factors influence cooking times and temperatures (e.g., time of year, intensity of sun).
• Expect cooking times at least double what you are used to.
• Put food out early, and don’t worry about it — solar cooking is easy!

Best Cooking Window:

• Foods cook fastest between 10 a.m. and 2 p.m. when the sun’s energy is most intense.

Cooking Equipment Recommendations:

• Use thin, black, metal pots with lids — these work best.
• Shallow, wide pots are better than tall, narrow ones (see page 26 for pot options).

Food Preparation Tips:

• The amount, volume, and height of food in the pot influence cooking speed.
• Small quantities of food, and food cut into small pieces, cook fastest.
• Food should be no deeper than a hand width.

What is a solar cooker?
A solar cooker is a device that allows you to cook food using the sun’s energy as fuel. It is possible to cook with the sun, and this guide will show you how.

Benefits of Solar Cooking:
• Saves time: Solar cooking reduces the need for constant monitoring or stirring.
• Reduces work and fuel use: No need to gather firewood or other fuels.
• Environmentally friendly: Uses renewable energy, reducing environmental impact.
• Retains flavor, moisture, and nutrients: Slow cooking helps retain food quality and makes meats tender.
• Healthier cooking: Recent studies suggest that foods cooked at moderate temperatures may be healthier.

How it works:
Solar cookers use the sun’s energy to slowly and gently cook all types of food — similar to boiling, roasting, or baking. Add approximately one hour to normal cook times when using a solar cooker. No need to watch, stir, or worry about food. The cook spends just two minutes: one to put food in and one to take it out.

Complements traditional cooking: Solar cookers can be used alongside traditional methods for night time or inclement weather.

Non-cooking uses: This guide also covers non-cooking applications such as solar water pasteurization — a basic survival skill.

Factors Affecting Cooking Time in a Solar Cooker:

• Time of year and day: Cooking times vary depending on the season and time of day.
• Amount of sun: More direct sunlight results in faster cooking.
• Amount of wind: High winds can slow down the cooking process.
• Thickness of pot: Thicker pots may take longer to heat up but retain heat better.
• Amount and size of food: Larger quantities or larger pieces of food will take longer to cook.
• Amount of water: More water increases cooking time due to higher thermal mass.

Typical Cooking Temperatures:
• Water boils at 100°C (212°F)
• Food cooks at 82–91°C (180–195°F)
• Pasteurization occurs at 71–82°C (160–165°F) for water and 49°C (120°F) for food
• Room temperature is approximately 22°C (72°F)

Typical Cooking Times for 4 Pounds (2 Kilograms) of Food on a Sunny Day:
• 1–2 hours: For foods that cook quickly, such as small vegetables or thin cuts of meat.
• 3–4 hours: For most common meals like rice, beans, and stews.
• 5–8 hours: For slow-cooking items like tough meats or large quantities of food.

2. Install the grate: Place a grate inside the furnace, ensuring it is positioned correctly for proper airflow.

How to Lay a Fire Using Different Methods

There are several methods for laying a fire. Three easy methods are:

1. Tepee Method: Arrange tinder and kindling in the shape of a cone. Light the center. As the cone burns, the outer logs will fall inward, feeding the heart of the fire. This method works well with wet wood.
2. Lean-To Method: Insert a green stick into the ground at a 30-degree angle, pointing downwind. Place tinder inside this lean-to stick and light it. As kindling catches, add more kindling.
3. Cross-Ditch Method: Scratch a cross about 1 foot in size in the ground, digging it 3 inches deep. Place a large wad of tinder in the middle of the cross. Build a kindling pyramid above the tinder. The shallow ditch allows air to sweep under the fire for a draft.

• Assign specific firefighting duties to individuals who are normally located near these points.

Firefighting Techniques:

Emergency Response:
• If a fire cannot be controlled with sand, get everyone outside and then use chemical extinguishers.
• Ensure that everyone understands the principle: in a choice between radiation and exploding gasoline, the radiation is the lesser evil.
